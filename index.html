About

I am a PhD candidate in World Economy at MGIMO University, studying the digital economy of the Gulf states and the political economy of digital transformation. My work sits at the intersection of world economy, technology markets, institutional change, and development policy, with a focus on how states and private capital build new digital growth models.

Alongside my academic work, I am the founder and CEO of WS Global, a technology firm specializing in enterprise architecture, applied AI, and digital transformation for civilian sectors (healthcare, higher education, and B2B). Building data-intensive enterprise products in practice shapes how I study technology in theory. My background in commercial IT architecture provides a grounded, engineering-level perspective on my academic research: how Gulf states attempt to build sovereign digital economies, finance tech sectors, and navigate global cloud dependencies.

Research

My current research examines how Persian Gulf countries develop digital sectors, attract technology investment, and reshape economic institutions through digital transformation. I am particularly interested in the role of state strategy, private capital, platform markets, and human capital in the formation of new digital growth models.

Publications

  1. 2026
    Rent Without Labour: AI Infrastructure and the Labour Test of Gulf Diversification. Peer-reviewed journal article. Innovation and Development (Taylor & Francis). Under review
    Executive summary
    • Introduces the labour test of Gulf AI diversification: the argument that the transition breaks the rentier structure only if it is measured by national labour absorbed into productive AI use, not by installed compute capacity.
    • Separates the AI economy into three layers (compute infrastructure, operations, productive use) and applies the test to Saudi Arabia and the UAE, finding that the public record measures capacity in detail but labour absorption barely at all.
  2. 2026
    Adopted but Not Authoritative: Conflict Deferral and AI Procurement in the Gulf. Peer-reviewed journal (Scopus-indexed). Under review
    Executive summary
    • Introduces conflict deferral, the temporal separation between acquiring an AI system and renegotiating the authority questions that genuine embedding would force open, to explain why world-class AI is widely adopted across the Gulf yet rarely enters the decision routines where authority actually sits.
    • Specifies five organisational entitlements (to decide, to make exceptions, to own data, to remain invisible, to bear responsibility for error) that operational embedding makes contestable, and argues that maturity lies not in acquiring more capability but in governing authority at the point of use.
  3. 2026
    Sovereign Capital and the Limits of Digital Sovereignty in the Gulf: Rentier States and the Foreclosed Conversion of Rent in the Digital Domain. Peer-reviewed journal (Scopus/WoS-indexed). Under review
    Executive summary
    • Demonstrates how the digital domain structurally forecloses classical rentier conversion, proving that sovereign wealth cannot purchase immunity from extraterritorial export controls and foreign intellectual property regimes.
    • Applies a three-layer model of the digital stack to show that GCC states achieve genuine legal sovereignty at the perimeter, while remaining structurally dependent at the compute and platform layers.
